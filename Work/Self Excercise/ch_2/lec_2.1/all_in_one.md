# Self Exercise - 2.1
## Q1. Define Probability in your own words.
**Answer:**  
Probability is a numerical measure of how likely an event is to occur. It quantifies uncertainty and ranges from impossibility to certainty.

---

## Q2. What is the range of probability values for any event?
**Answer:**  
The value of probability always lies between **0 and 1**, inclusive.  
- 0 means the event will never happen  
- 1 means the event will definitely happen

---

## Q3. How is probability used in real-life data science scenarios?
**Answer:**  
In data science, probability is used for:
- Predicting outcomes (e.g., customer churn, stock prices)
- Handling uncertainty in models
- Risk assessment
- Classification and regression algorithms
- A/B testing and hypothesis testing

---

## Q4. Explain the difference between random experiment and deterministic event.
**Answer:**  
- **Random experiment:** An experiment whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty (e.g., tossing a coin).
- **Deterministic event:** An event whose outcome is fixed and predictable (e.g., 2 + 2 = 4).

---

## Q5. What is a sample space? Give an example.
**Answer:**  
A sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment.

**Example:**  
For tossing a coin:  
S = {Heads, Tails}

---

## Q6. Define the term event and list its types.
**Answer:**  
An event is a subset of a sample space.

**Types of events:**
- Simple event  
- Compound event  
- Impossible event  
- Sure event  
- Independent event  
- Dependent event  
- Mutually exclusive event

---

## Q7. What do the terms favorable outcomes and total outcomes mean?
**Answer:**  
- **Favorable outcomes:** Outcomes that satisfy the event of interest  
- **Total outcomes:** All possible outcomes in the sample space

---

## Q8. Define independent and dependent events with examples.
**Answer:**  
- **Independent events:** Occurrence of one does not affect the other  
  - Example: Tossing two coins
- **Dependent events:** Occurrence of one affects the probability of the other  
  - Example: Drawing two cards without replacement

---

## Q9. What is a mutually exclusive event?
**Answer:**  
Mutually exclusive events are events that cannot occur at the same time.

**Example:**  
In a single coin toss, getting Heads and Tails together is impossible.

---

## Q10. If a coin is tossed once, what is the probability of getting heads?
**Answer:**  
Total outcomes = 2  
Favorable outcomes = 1  
Probability = 1/2

---

## Q11. If a dice is rolled, what is the probability of getting an even number?
**Answer:**  
Even numbers = {2, 4, 6}  
Total outcomes = 6  
Probability = 3/6 = 1/2

---

## Q12. What is the probability of drawing a red card from a standard deck?
**Answer:**  
Red cards = 26  
Total cards = 52  
Probability = 26/52 = 1/2

---

## Q13. A bag has 4 red and 6 blue balls. What is the probability of picking a red ball?
**Answer:**  
Total balls = 10  
Red balls = 4  
Probability = 4/10 = 2/5

---

## Q14. What are the different types of probability events?
**Answer:**  
- Simple events  
- Compound events  
- Independent events  
- Dependent events  
- Mutually exclusive events

---

## Q15. What is empirical probability? Provide a real-world example.
**Answer:**  
Empirical probability is based on actual experiments or observations.

**Example:**  
If it rains 20 days out of 100 days,  
Probability of rain = 20/100 = 0.2

---

## Q16. What is theoretical probability? How is it different from empirical?
**Answer:**  
Theoretical probability is calculated using mathematical reasoning, assuming all outcomes are equally likely.

**Difference:**  
- Theoretical: Based on theory  
- Empirical: Based on real data

---

## Q17. When is empirical probability more useful than theoretical probability?
**Answer:**  
Empirical probability is more useful when:
- Outcomes are not equally likely
- Real-world data is available
- Theoretical assumptions are unrealistic

---

## Q18. Compare empirical and theoretical probability with one practical example.
**Answer:**  
**Example:** Coin toss  
- Theoretical probability of heads = 1/2  
- Empirical probability may differ based on actual results from many tosses
